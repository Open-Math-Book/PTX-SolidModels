Existence. To show existence, suppose that there is some integer that cannot be written as the product of primes. Let \(S\) be the set of all such numbers. By the Principle of Well-Ordering, \(S\) has a smallest number, say \(a\text{.}\). If the only positive factors of \(a\) are \(a\) and 1, then \(a\) is prime, which is a contradiction. Hence, \(a = a_1 a_2\) where \(1 \lt a_1 \lt a\) and \(1 \lt a_2 \lt a\text{.}\). Neither \(a_1\in S\) nor \(a_2 \in S\text{,}\), since \(a\) is the smallest element in \(S\text{.}\). So \begin{align*} a_1 & = p_1 \cdots p_r\\ a_2 & = q_1 \cdots q_s. \end{align*}
