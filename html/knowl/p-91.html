Suppose that \(S = \{ 1,2,3 \}\text{.}\). Define a map \(\pi :S\rightarrow S\) by \begin{equation*} \pi( 1 ) = 2, \qquad \pi( 2 ) = 1, \qquad \pi( 3 ) = 3. \end{equation*} This is a bijective map. An alternative way to write \(\pi\) is \begin{equation*} \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 2 & 3 \\ \pi(1) & \pi(2) & \pi(3) \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 2 & 3 \\ 2 & 1 & 3 \end{pmatrix}. \end{equation*} For any set \(S\text{,}\), a one-to-one and onto mapping \(\pi : S \rightarrow S\) is called a permutation of \(S\text{.}\).
