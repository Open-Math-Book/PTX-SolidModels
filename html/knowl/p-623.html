Nonzero complex numbers can also be represented using polar coordinates. To specify any nonzero point on the plane, it suffices to give an angle \(\theta\) from the positive \(x\) axis in the counterclockwise direction and a distance \(r\) from the origin, as in FigureÂ 4.0.18. We can see that \begin{equation*} z = a + bi = r( \cos \theta + i \sin \theta). \end{equation*} Hence, \begin{equation*} r = |z| = \sqrt{a^2 + b^2} \end{equation*} and \begin{align*} a & = r \cos \theta\\ b & = r \sin \theta. \end{align*} We sometimes abbreviate \(r( \cos \theta + i \sin \theta)\) as \(r \cis \theta\text{.}\). To assure that the representation of \(z\) is well-defined, we also require that \(0^{\circ} \leq \theta \lt 360^{\circ}\text{.}\). If the measurement is in radians, then \(0 \leq \theta \lt2 \pi\text{.}\).
