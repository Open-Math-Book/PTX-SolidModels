The laws of exponents still work in \({\mathbb Z}_n\text{;}\); that is, if \(b \equiv a^x \pmod{ n}\) and \(c \equiv a^y \pmod{ n}\text{,}\), then \(bc \equiv a^{x+y} \pmod{ n}\text{.}\). We can compute \(a^{2^k} \pmod{ n}\) in \(k\) multiplications by computing \begin{gather*} a^{2^0} \pmod{ n}\\ a^{2^1} \pmod{ n }\\ \vdots\\ a^{2^k} \pmod{ n}. \end{gather*} Each step involves squaring the answer obtained in the previous step, dividing by \(n\text{,}\), and taking the remainder.
